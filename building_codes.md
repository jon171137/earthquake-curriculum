# Building Codes

## Overview
Seismic building codes are technical standards that specify how structures must be designed and constructed to withstand earthquake forces. These codes are the result of decades of earthquake engineering research and lessons learned from earthquakes that exposed structural weaknesses. Building codes save lives by requiring structures to resist earthquake ground motion, remain standing, and allow occupants to evacuate safely. Modern building codes have evolved significantly, now incorporating performance-based design principles aimed at not only preventing collapse but also minimizing economic losses and enabling rapid recovery after earthquakes.

## Content

### Historical Evolution of Seismic Building Codes

Seismic design provisions in building codes have developed largely in response to catastrophic earthquakes that revealed structural vulnerabilities:

#### Early Development (Pre-1960)
- **1755 Lisbon Earthquake**: Prompted early considerations of earthquake resistance in architectural design, though systematic building codes did not yet exist
- **Early 20th Century**: Initial seismic design provisions were developed but lacked scientific foundation and were often inconsistent

#### Post-1960 Development
- **1960 Chile Earthquake (M9.5)**: Motivated Chile to develop rigorous seismic building codes and set a global example
- **SEAOC Blue Book (1960s)**: The **Structural Engineers Association of California** published the Blue Book, which became the foundation for seismic building code development in the United States and influenced international standards
- **Development Focus**: Early codes established the concept of earthquake zones, seismic coefficients, and basic lateral force design principles

#### 1971 San Fernando Earthquake Revisions
- The **1971 San Fernando Earthquake (M6.7)** revealed failures in reinforced concrete structures
- Building codes were completely revised to incorporate new concrete design and detailing provisions
- Emphasis shifted to ensuring **ductility** (the ability to bend and flex without breaking) in concrete members

#### 1994 Northridge Earthquake Refinements
- The **1994 Northridge Earthquake (M6.7)** exposed serious deficiencies in **steel moment frame** construction
- Field investigations revealed that welded connections were prone to brittle fracture during strong shaking
- **FEMA 350–354** recommendations were developed and subsequently incorporated into model codes and steel industry standards
- Building codes were revised to include more rigorous connection detailing requirements and quality control provisions

#### Modern Code Development
- **NEHRP Recommended Seismic Provisions**: The **National Earthquake Hazards Reduction Program** develops recommended seismic provisions that serve as the basis for the **ASCE 7** design standard
- **International Building Code (IBC)**: These provisions are incorporated into the IBC, adopted by most U.S. states and many countries
- **Performance-Based Seismic Design (PBSD)**: Emerging approach that explicitly defines performance objectives (e.g., "no damage during moderate earthquakes," "avoid collapse during rare earthquakes") rather than prescriptive design parameters

### Key Principles of Seismic Building Code Design

#### 1. Seismic Force Reduction through Ductility
- **Elastic vs. Inelastic Design**: Modern codes do not require buildings to remain completely elastic (rigid) during strong earthquakes. Instead, codes allow **controlled inelastic deformation** (bending, deformation) if the structure remains stable and can dissipate energy without collapse.
- **Response Reduction Factors**: Codes employ "response reduction factors" (R-factors) that reduce design forces based on the expected ductility and energy dissipation capability of a structure. Higher R-factors are applied to more ductile systems.
- **Ductility Requirements**: Codes specify reinforcement detailing, concrete strength, and member configuration to ensure structures can deform significantly before failing.

#### 2. Redundancy and Multiple Load Paths
- **Structural Redundancy**: Codes require multiple, independent load-carrying paths so that failure of one element does not lead to catastrophic collapse of the entire structure.
- **Continuous Connections**: Structural elements must be effectively connected to each other to transfer forces through multiple pathways.

#### 3. Damping Considerations
- **Inherent Damping**: Codes account for the energy dissipation that occurs naturally in buildings as they vibrate and deform.
- **Added Damping Systems**: Modern designs may include **tuned mass dampers**, **viscous dampers**, **friction dampers**, or **base isolators** to reduce building motion and acceleration during earthquakes.

#### 4. Seismic Design Categories
- **Classification**: Buildings are assigned to Seismic Design Categories (A–F, depending on seismic hazard level and occupancy) that determine the rigor of design requirements.
- **Occupancy Categories**: Critical facilities (hospitals, fire stations, emergency centers) are assigned higher categories and require more stringent design standards than ordinary office buildings.
- **Essential Facilities**: Must be designed to remain operational after an earthquake and typically require increased design forces.

#### 5. Ground Motion Selection
- **Hazard Levels**: Modern codes specify two or more earthquake hazard levels:
  - **Design Basis Earthquake (DBE)** or **BSE-1**: Earthquake with ~10% probability of exceedance in 50 years (i.e., a 475-year return period earthquake)
  - **Maximum Considered Earthquake (MCE)** or **BSE-2**: Larger earthquake with ~2% probability of exceedance in 50 years (i.e., a 2,475-year return period earthquake)
- **Site-Specific Hazard Assessment**: For critical and essential facilities, site-specific ground-motion studies are required, incorporating local geology, fault rupture geometry, and soil characteristics

#### 6. Nonstructural Elements
- **Contents and Equipment**: Modern codes increasingly address damage to nonstructural elements—mechanical systems, electrical equipment, ceiling systems, partition walls—which can be more costly to repair than structural damage and disrupt building operations
- **Acceleration-Based Design**: Nonstructural design forces are based on floor acceleration amplification rather than building-wide seismic coefficients

### Material-Specific Design Provisions

#### Reinforced Concrete Buildings
- **Ductile Detailing**: Transverse reinforcement (stirrups) must be closely spaced to prevent premature shear failure and confine concrete, improving ductility
- **Coupling Beams**: In shear wall buildings, coupling beams between walls must have adequate strength and deformation capacity
- **Bond Development**: Reinforcing bars must be properly anchored (developed) to prevent pullout
- **Special Moment-Resisting Frames (SMRFs)**: Stringent requirements for column and beam design, joint detailing, and connection strength

#### Steel Buildings
- **Connection Design**: Post-Northridge, welded connections require special qualification, testing, and inspection protocols to prevent brittle fracture
- **Column Base Plates**: Design to allow plastic hinges to form in beams rather than columns
- **Lateral Bracing**: Adequate bracing to prevent member buckling under compressive forces
- **Damping**: Some modern steel designs incorporate viscous or friction dampers to reduce motion and stress

#### Wood Construction
- **Fastener Design**: Nails, bolts, and connectors must be sized and spaced to ensure adequate strength and prevent splitting
- **Diaphragm Continuity**: Roof and floor diaphragms must be effectively connected and braced
- **Foundation Anchorage**: Wood-frame structures must be anchored to their foundations to prevent sliding

#### Unreinforced Masonry (URM) Buildings
- **Retrofit Requirements**: Older unreinforced masonry buildings (predominant in many urban centers, particularly in the Northeast, West Coast, and Europe) are extremely seismically vulnerable and subject to mandatory retrofitting programs in many jurisdictions
- **Wall Anchorage**: Masonry walls must be anchored to floors and roofs
- **Out-of-Plane Bracing**: Horizontal bracing prevents walls from bowing outward (pancaking)
- **Diaphragm Strengthening**: Floors and roofs are reinforced with plywood sheathing or similar materials
- **Retrofit Costs**: Bringing URM buildings up to modern standards can cost $6–30+ per square foot

### Seismic Retrofitting Programs

Many jurisdictions have implemented mandatory seismic retrofitting programs targeting the most vulnerable building stock:

#### Soft-Story Buildings
- **Problem**: Multi-story residential buildings with open ground floors (retail storefronts, parking) are prone to "pancaking" collapse of upper stories
- **Retrofit Requirements**: Steel bracing or shear walls added to provide lateral support; examples include San Francisco's mandatory retrofit program (2017)
- **Cost**: $10,000–$30,000+ per unit

#### Unreinforced Masonry Parapets
- **Problem**: Heavy ornamental masonry parapets can fall and strike pedestrians or vehicles below
- **Retrofit**: Removal, tie-down, or bracing; quick and cost-effective

#### Pre-1975 Steel-Frame Buildings
- **Problem**: Welded connections in older steel buildings can fail in earthquakes; post-Northridge buildings require enhanced connection design
- **Retrofit**: Connection upgrades, supplemental bracing, or damping systems

#### Hospital and Essential Facility Upgrades
- **Requirements**: Many jurisdictions mandate that hospitals and emergency facilities be upgraded to Occupancy Category IV standards
- **Goals**: Ensure continued operation after earthquakes to support disaster response

### Performance-Based Design and the Future of Seismic Codes

#### Moving Beyond Prescriptive Standards
- **Traditional Approach**: Prescriptive codes specify design methods, materials, and procedures that buildings must follow
- **Performance-Based Approach**: Define performance objectives (e.g., "remain operational," "repairable damage only," "life safety") and allow engineers to design solutions that meet those objectives

#### Performance Objectives
- **Operational**: Buildings remain functional with minimal damage; systems continue to operate
- **Immediate Occupancy**: Building is safe to occupy and can be reoccupied with minor repairs
- **Life Safety**: Structure remains standing and occupants can evacuate safely; damage may be significant but collapse is prevented
- **Collapse Prevention**: Structure remains standing; significant damage and casualties may occur, but global collapse is prevented

#### Functional Recovery
- **New Focus Area**: Beyond safety, codes are increasingly concerned with **how quickly buildings and infrastructure can return to function** after an earthquake
- **Rapid Reoccupancy**: Design strategies to minimize damage, facilitate repairs, and speed recovery
- **Lifeline Protection**: Ensuring utility systems (power, water, gas, communications) remain operational or quickly restored

### International Building Code Standards

#### United States
- **IBC (International Building Code)**: Model code adopted by most states; incorporates ASCE 7 seismic provisions
- **ASCE 7**: Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures
- **ACI 318**: Building Code Requirements for Structural Concrete
- **AISC 341**: Seismic Provisions for Structural Steel Buildings

#### Europe
- **Eurocode 8 (EN 1998-1)**: Seismic design code for Europe; requires higher design forces and stricter detailing than many U.S. codes in some cases

#### New Zealand
- **NZS 1170.5**: New Zealand Standard for Seismic Design; particularly stringent given the country's high seismicity

#### Japan
- **Building Standard Law**: Specifies rigorous seismic requirements, incorporating lessons from 1995 Kobe and 2011 Tōhoku earthquakes

#### Chile
- **NCh433 Of.96 Mod.2009**: Chilean seismic code; developed following the devastating 1960 M9.5 earthquake; one of the world's most advanced codes

#### Turkey
- **Turkish Building Earthquake Code (2018)**: Updated following the 1999 İzmit earthquake; further revised in 2024–2025 following the 2023 Kahramanmaraş earthquakes

### Barriers to Implementation

Despite advanced building codes, several factors limit their effectiveness:

- **Non-Compliance**: Developers and builders sometimes use substandard materials or inadequate detailing to reduce costs
- **Lack of Inspection**: Insufficient inspection and enforcement during construction
- **Code Adoption Lag**: Some jurisdictions adopt outdated model codes; comprehensive updates can lag 10+ years
- **Cost Concerns**: Seismic design increases construction costs (typically 5–15% for new buildings, more for retrofits), creating pressure to cut corners
- **Historic Preservation**: Retrofitting historic structures to modern codes can damage their character or is economically infeasible
- **Existing Building Stock**: The majority of buildings in seismically active regions were constructed before modern codes existed; retrofitting all of them is prohibitively expensive
- **Post-Earthquake Compliance**: After disasters, reconstruction is sometimes rushed, and code compliance may be compromised in the rush to rebuild
