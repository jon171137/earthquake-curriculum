# Emergency Response

## Overview
Emergency response to earthquakes involves coordinated, rapid actions by first responders, emergency managers, medical personnel, and community volunteers to save lives, provide immediate aid, and stabilize the situation. Effective response requires advance planning, training, coordination among agencies, and clear lines of command. The response phase typically lasts 0–10 days following the earthquake, during which the primary goals are rescue operations, medical care, preventing aftershock injuries, controlling fires, and restoring critical services. A well-executed response can dramatically reduce casualties and facilitate faster recovery.

## Content

### Phases of Post-Earthquake Operations

Post-earthquake activities occur in three major phases, each with distinct goals and timelines:

#### Phase 1: Response and Relief (0–25 days)
**Goals**: Rescue survivors, stabilize the situation, prevent further loss of life, provide emergency aid

**Timeline**:
- **Immediate Response (0–10 days)**: Initial rescue operations, emergency medical care, assessment of structural dangers
- **Emergency Relief (0–25 days)**: Provision of food, water, temporary shelter, identification and care for the dead, restoration of critical services

**Key Activities**:
- Search and rescue of trapped persons
- Triage and emergency medical treatment
- Firefighting and hazard control
- Damage assessment and identification of unsafe structures
- Restoring power, water, and communication systems
- Establishing emergency shelters and food distribution
- Managing evacuations and crowds
- Accounting for the dead

#### Phase 2: Recovery (Days to Months)
**Goals**: Return to relatively normal conditions, restore services, begin reconstruction planning

**Timeline**: Weeks to months following the earthquake

**Key Activities**:
- Transition of temporary facilities (water, electricity) to longer-term solutions
- Reopening of schools and offices
- Restoration of basic economic activities (commerce, production, transportation)
- Repair of main roads, utilities, and transportation networks
- Detailed damage and loss assessments
- Development of recovery plans and financing mechanisms
- Rebuilding of partially damaged structures

#### Phase 3: Reconstruction and Long-Term Recovery (Months to Years)
**Goals**: Return to pre-earthquake conditions or better, incorporate lessons learned

**Timeline**: Months to years

**Key Activities**:
- Major infrastructure reconstruction
- Building code updates and enforcement
- Implementation of resilience improvements
- Implementation of community recovery projects
- Long-term economic and social recovery

### Immediate Response Operations

#### Search and Rescue (SAR)

**Overview**
Search and rescue operations are critical to saving lives in the first 72 hours following an earthquake, often called the "golden period." The vast majority of survivors who are extricated from collapsed buildings are rescued within the first 72 hours. After this period, survival rates drop dramatically.

**Key Operational Phases**

1. **Location of Victims**
   - **Visual Inspection**: Survivors may be visible or audible
   - **Sound-Detecting Devices**: Listening devices can detect victims who retain consciousness and can produce sounds (tapping, calling)
   - **Search Dogs**: Trained canines can detect scent of humans buried under debris
   - **Technology**: Thermal imaging, seismic sensors, ground-penetrating radar for locating voids that may contain survivors
   - **Canine Search Teams**: Often can locate survivors before mechanical excavation, minimizing additional building collapses

2. **Penetration Techniques**
   - **Horizontal Penetration**: Rescue teams move laterally through building debris along floor levels. This method is ergonomically favorable and minimizes risk to trapped persons. Tunnel width should be at least 1.2 meters to allow two rescuers to work simultaneously.
   - **Vertical Penetration**: Teams work downward through collapsed floors to reach victims below
   - **Cautious Advancement**: Teams must work carefully to avoid triggering secondary collapses that could injure rescuers and trapped victims

3. **Victim Extraction and Medical Care**
   - **Careful Removal**: Victims must be carefully extricated to avoid exacerbating injuries
   - **On-Site Medical Care**: Initial assessment and stabilization of injuries is provided before evacuation
   - **Transport Priority**: Victims are triaged (prioritized based on injury severity) for transport to hospitals
   - **Crush Injuries**: Special protocols for crush syndrome (rhabdomyolysis from prolonged crushing) require careful fluid management to prevent kidney failure

**Resource Requirements**
- Urban Search and Rescue (USAR) teams trained in rescue techniques
- Heavy equipment: cranes, excavators, shoring materials
- Safety equipment: helmets, gloves, dust masks, protective eyewear
- Medical personnel embedded within rescue teams
- Canine teams
- Communication equipment

#### Emergency Medical Response

**Medical Triage**
When hospitals become overwhelmed with casualties, rapid triage systems are essential to allocate limited resources to patients with the highest likelihood of survival:

- **Red Tag**: Immediate life-threatening injuries requiring urgent treatment (internal bleeding, severe respiratory distress, critical head injuries)
- **Yellow Tag**: Serious but stable injuries; can wait hours for treatment (moderate burns, fractures with intact blood supply)
- **Green Tag**: Minor injuries; can self-treat or wait (minor wounds, sprains, psychological distress)
- **Black Tag**: Deceased or expectant (unsurvivable injuries); comfort care only

**Common Earthquake Injuries**
- **Crush Injuries**: From collapsed buildings; can cause compartment syndrome and rhabdomyolysis
- **Lacerations and Puncture Wounds**: From broken glass, metal, and concrete
- **Fractures**: Limb fractures from crush or impact; spinal fractures from falling objects or building collapse
- **Blunt Trauma**: Bruising, internal bleeding from impact with debris
- **Psychological Trauma**: PTSD, acute stress disorder, depression in survivors and responders
- **Epidemiologic Shifts**: Post-earthquake outbreaks of infectious disease in crowded shelters, contaminated water systems, and poor sanitation

**Medical Challenges**
- **Hospital Damage**: Hospitals themselves may be damaged, reducing treatment capacity precisely when it is most needed
- **Medical Supply Shortages**: Disruption to supply chains limits availability of medications, blood, surgical supplies
- **Staff Casualties**: Medical personnel may themselves be injured or displaced
- **Infrastructure Loss**: Loss of power, water, and sterilization capabilities complicates medical care
- **Communication Failures**: Disrupted communication makes coordination difficult

#### Fire and Hazard Control

**Fire Response**
Earthquakes often trigger fires through:
- Ruptured gas lines igniting electrical sparks
- Overturned stoves and fireplaces
- Electrical short circuits from damaged wiring
- Chemical reactions from spilled materials

**Earthquake-Specific Fire Challenges**
- **Water System Failure**: Broken water mains reduce water pressure, limiting firefighting capability
- **Access Difficulty**: Damaged roads and debris block fire truck access
- **Widespread Fires**: Multiple fires may overwhelm response capacity
- **Aftershocks**: Firefighting operations must be conducted carefully as aftershocks can cause additional collapses

**Post-Earthquake Fire Examples**
- **1906 San Francisco Earthquake**: Fires burned for 3 days, destroying most of the city; 90% of damage was from fire, not earthquake shaking
- **1985 Mexico City Earthquake**: Industrial fires in damaged refineries and chemical plants threatened contamination and explosion hazards

#### Structural Damage Assessment

**Yellow Tag (Inspected—Damaged, Unsafe)**
Structures with major damage and deemed unsafe for entry. Restricted access until repairs are completed.

**Red Tag (Dangerous)**
Structures with severe damage and immediate collapse risk. No entry permitted. Demolition may be necessary.

**Green Tag (Safe)**
Structures with minor or no damage. Occupancy permitted.

**Post-Earthquake Structural Assessment Process**
- Initial visual inspection by trained engineers
- Identification of obvious hazards (leaning walls, cracks, missing structural elements)
- Evaluation of surrounding hazards (utility lines, debris, unstable adjacent buildings)
- Determination of occupancy status based on structural safety
- Detailed engineering assessment for yellow-tagged buildings to determine repairability

**ATC-20 Rapid Evaluation Form** (commonly used in the US)
Standardized one-page form used by engineers to rapidly evaluate building safety post-earthquake. Provides visual reference for common damage patterns and safety concerns.

### Disaster Management Coordination

#### Emergency Operations Center (EOC)
**Function**: Central location for information gathering, disaster analysis, and response coordination

**Operations**:
- Collects damage reports from field teams
- Coordinates dispatch of resources
- Issues emergency warnings and public information
- Coordinates with state and federal agencies
- Tracks resource utilization and shortages
- Makes decisions about evacuation orders, shelter operations, and recovery priorities

**Staffing**:
- Emergency manager (director/coordinator)
- Operations section chief (coordinates rescue, medical response, fire response)
- Planning section chief (develops action plans, manages information)
- Logistics section chief (manages resource allocation and supply chains)
- Finance/administration section chief (manages financial and administrative aspects)
- Representatives from police, fire, public health, utilities, transportation, military

#### Unified Command
**Principle**: Single incident commander or unified command structure establishes clear lines of authority and decision-making

**Benefits**:
- Clear accountability
- Avoids duplication or gaps in operations
- Enables rapid decision-making
- Coordinates multiple agencies with different expertise

#### Inter-Agency Coordination
- **Local Response**: City/county emergency management leads initial response
- **State Assistance**: State emergency management and National Guard provide additional resources
- **Federal Response**: FEMA, Department of Defense, specialized federal agencies provide assistance if local and state resources are overwhelmed
- **International Assistance**: For very large earthquakes, international aid may be requested

**Challenges**:
- Communication difficulties (phone systems overwhelmed, radio frequencies congested)
- Jurisdictional boundaries (earthquake damage may affect multiple counties/states)
- Different agency protocols and terminology
- Competition for limited resources

### Public Communications and Information

#### Early Warnings
- **Earthquake Early Warning (EEW)**: Systems that detect earthquakes and broadcast alerts within seconds of earthquake detection, before strong shaking arrives at distant locations (provides 5–60 seconds of warning, depending on distance)
- **Tsunami Warnings**: Alerts for coastal areas threatened by earthquake-generated tsunamis
- **Aftershock Warnings**: Alerts regarding expected aftershock activity
- **Hazard Alerts**: Warnings about utility disruptions, road closures, hazardous materials releases, or other ongoing dangers

#### Public Messaging
- **Accurate Information**: Regular, accurate updates prevent rumors and panic
- **Clear Instructions**: Guidance on evacuation routes, shelter locations, water distribution points, recovery assistance
- **Consistent Messaging**: Coordination among agencies to avoid conflicting information
- **Multiple Channels**: Use of emergency alert systems, social media, broadcast media, door-to-door notification

#### Information Management
- **Centralized Information**: Single source of truth regarding casualty counts, damage estimates, shelter availability, recovery services
- **Transparent Reporting**: Acknowledging uncertainty while providing best-available information
- **Frequent Updates**: Information provided frequently to prevent information vacuums that breeding grounds for rumors

### Post-Earthquake Damage and Loss Assessment

**Overview**
Rapid and detailed assessment of earthquake damage is essential for:
- Estimating recovery needs and costs
- Determining resource allocation priorities
- Guiding reconstruction planning
- Informing insurance and humanitarian assistance
- Updating hazard models and building code provisions

**Assessment Process**
1. **Rapid Impact Assessment (0–25 days)**: Initial damage survey to estimate impact scope, identify critical needs, guide emergency response
2. **Detailed Damage Assessment (weeks to months)**: Systematic evaluation of each affected building and infrastructure system
3. **Loss Assessment**: Estimation of direct costs (building damage, infrastructure damage) and indirect costs (business interruption, lost economic activity)

**Building-Specific Damage Evaluation**
- **Structural Elements**: Columns, beams, walls, foundation integrity
- **Nonstructural Elements**: Partitions, ceilings, equipment, utilities
- **Building Systems**: HVAC, electrical, plumbing, fire systems
- **Functionality**: Can the building resume operations? If not, what repairs are needed?

### Recovery Timeline

**0–10 Days**: Rescue operations dominate. Mortality peaks at day 2–3, then declines rapidly after day 72.

**10–25 Days**: Transition from rescue to relief. Restoration of critical services begins. Temporary shelters and food distribution established.

**25 Days–Few Weeks**: Recovery operations in full swing. Schools and offices reopen. Utility restoration accelerates. Damaged structure demolition or stabilization ongoing.

**Weeks to Months**: Infrastructure reconstruction accelerates. Housing reconstruction begins. Economic activity gradually returns to normal levels. Planning for long-term reconstruction advances.

**Months to Years**: Large-scale reconstruction. Implementation of resilience improvements. Return to normal operations or improved conditions.

**Recovery Trajectory Example (from 2014 South Napa, CA Earthquake)**:
- ~90% of white-tagged (safe) buildings recovered within 6 months
- ~83% of white-tagged buildings fully recovered within 18 months
- ~50% of red-tagged (dangerous) buildings were still not recovered at 18 months
- ~80% of non-seismically designed structures sustained significant damage

### Special Response Considerations

#### Psychological and Mental Health Response
- **Acute Stress Disorder**: Common immediate reaction; usually resolves within weeks
- **PTSD**: Can develop in survivors with serious injuries, loss of family members, or pre-existing trauma
- **Response Personnel**: First responders and emergency workers experience high rates of psychological trauma
- **Vulnerable Populations**: Children, elderly, and persons with pre-existing mental illness are at elevated risk
- **Support Services**: Mental health counseling, support groups, crisis hotlines essential

#### Vulnerable Populations
- **Elderly**: May have difficulty evacuating, accessing services, or adapting to temporary shelters
- **Persons with Disabilities**: May have mobility limitations, communication barriers, or dependence on electricity-dependent medical equipment
- **Non-English Speakers**: May lack access to information and services
- **Homeless and Low-Income Populations**: May lack resources for recovery and are vulnerable to exploitation

#### International Disaster Assistance
- **Red Cross/Red Crescent**: Provides humanitarian aid, medical assistance, disaster relief
- **United Nations**: Coordinates international response for very large earthquakes
- **Foreign Urban Search and Rescue Teams**: Deploy to provide rescue assistance
- **Financial Assistance**: International donations and loans support recovery
