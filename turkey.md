# Turkey

## Overview

With approximately 1,200â€“1,500 km of active faulting, Turkey experiences frequent and often destructive earthquakes. ## Content

### Tectonic Setting
Turkey's earthquakes result from complex plate interactions and large fault systems:
- **North Anatolian Fault (NAF)**: A right-lateral strike-slip fault extending approximately 1,200â€“1,500 km along northern Turkey, from the Karliova triple junction in the east to the Aegean Sea in the west.
  - Slip rate: Approximately 2.0â€“2.5 cm per year, making it one of the world's most actively slipping faults.
  - Similar in many ways to the San Andreas Fault in California.
  - Runs about 20 km south of Istanbul, Turkey's largest and most economically important city.
- **East Anatolian Fault**: A secondary transform boundary in southeastern Turkey.
- **Sea of Marmara**: An extensional basin formed by a releasing bend in the strike-slip system; similar to the Salton Trough in California. This basin poses a significant seismic gap.
- **Plate Boundaries**: The NAF represents the transform boundary between the Eurasian Plate to the north and the Anatolian sub-plate to the south, which is itself under compressional stress from African-Eurasian plate interactions.

### Major Historical Earthquakes

#### 1939 Erzincan Earthquake (M7.8â€“7.9)
- **Date**: December 26, 1939
- **Location**: Erzincan, northeastern Turkey
- **Magnitude**: M7.8â€“7.9
- **Deaths**: 32,700+ killed; 100,000+ injured
- **Significance**: The most deadly earthquake in Turkey's recorded history; triggered the "westward-migrating" earthquake sequence along the North Anatolian Fault
- **Pattern Discovery**: This earthquake began a remarkable pattern of successive ruptures migrating progressively westward along the NAF, each potentially triggering the next

#### 1940s Westward-Migrating Sequence
Following the 1939 Erzincan earthquake, a series of large earthquakes ruptured different segments of the NAF moving progressively westward:
- **1942 Niksar-Erbaa** (M7.0): ~3,000 dead
- **1943 Tosya-Ladik** (M7.2): 2,824 dead
- **1944 Bolu-Gerede** (M7.2): 3,959 dead

This sequence provided seismologists with unprecedented understanding of how earthquakes can trigger subsequent ruptures along a major fault system.

### Infrastructure Vulnerabilities
- **Building Code Non-Compliance**: Despite building code improvements post-1999, the 2023 earthquake revealed that many buildings were constructed with substandard materials, inadequate reinforcement, and non-compliant design.
- **Soft-Story Buildings**: Multi-story residential buildings with ground-floor commercial spaces (soft-story configurations) are particularly vulnerable to collapse.
- **Older Structures**: Pre-1999 buildings designed without modern seismic standards remain prevalent and highly vulnerable.
- **Unreinforced Masonry**: Older masonry structures collapse readily in strong shaking.
- **Urban Density**: Major population centers like Istanbul, Ankara, and Gaziantep are concentrated in seismically active areas.
- **Lifeline Systems**: Water, sewer, gas, electricity, and communication networks are vulnerable to disruption.
- **Industrial Infrastructure**: Critical industries, refineries, and chemical plants are located near major fault systems.
- **Sea of Marmara Seismic Gap**: The gap near Istanbul poses a threat to Turkey's most important city and could generate a major earthquake at any time.

### Seismic Risk Assessment
- **Earthquake Frequency**: Turkey experiences hundreds of earthquakes annually; damaging earthquakes (M6+) occur regularly; events of M7+ magnitude have occurred about once per decade on average historically.
- **North Anatolian Fault Threat**: The westernmost portions of the NAF, including the Sea of Marmara segment, are considered to pose the greatest risk. The Istanbul metropolitan area of over 15 million people is within striking distance of a potential M7+ rupture.
- **Eastern Faults**: The East Anatolian Fault and associated structures in southeastern Turkey pose ongoing hazard.
- **Slow-Slip Events**: Some segments show evidence of slow-slip behavior, complicating earthquake prediction.
- **Current Gap**: Following the 2023 ruptures, the Sea of Marmara and western portions of the NAF remain as potential future sources.

### Preparedness Efforts
- **Building Codes**: Turkey updated its building regulations following the 1999 earthquake, establishing the Turkish Earthquake Regulation (2007). The Turkish Building Earthquake Code (2018) introduced even more rigorous design criteria.
- **2019 Code Updates**: The most recent code updates in 2019 addressed four additional building types: high-rise, seismically isolated, cold-formed steel, and wooden structures.
- **2024-2025 Reforms**: Following the 2023 earthquake, Turkey has implemented significant legal and regulatory reforms:
  - Stricter urban transformation and retrofitting requirements for at-risk buildings
  - Enhanced financial incentives (zero-interest loans, relocation subsidies)
  - Mandatory integration of green building practices and sustainable construction
  - Stricter oversight mechanisms to prevent delays and ensure code compliance
  - Expedited project timelines and enhanced stakeholder engagement
- **Building Inspection**: Stricter inspection protocols and integration of seismic risk assessments into urban planning
- **Earthquake Early Warning**: Development of early warning systems to provide alerts to critical infrastructure and the public
- **Public Education**: National earthquake awareness campaigns emphasizing preparedness and evacuation procedures
- **Research & Monitoring**: Continuous seismic monitoring and paleoseismic research to understand fault behavior and improve hazard forecasts
- **International Collaboration**: Participation in international earthquake research networks and disaster response frameworks

### Recent Activity
- Frequent small to moderate earthquakes (M2â€“M5) occur throughout Turkey annually
- Ongoing aftershock activity from the 2023 earthquake sequence continues in the affected region
- Continuous monitoring of the North Anatolian Fault, particularly the Sea of Marmara segment
- Concern about potential rupture of the Istanbul Gap, where no major earthquake has occurred since 1766
